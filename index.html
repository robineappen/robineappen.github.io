My Work

My research journey explores the fascinating formation and evolution of galaxies under the framework of Modified Newtonian Dynamics (MOND), challenging the conventional dark matter paradigm.

In my first major study, "The formation of early-type galaxies in Milgromian Dynamics" (Eappen et al. 2022), I simulated the monolithic collapse of post-Big Bang gas clouds in a MONDian universe. These simulations successfully recreated properties of observed early-type galaxies (ETGs), showing that Milgromian gravity can naturally form elliptical galaxies with realistic structural and kinematic features.

Building on this, my second study, "The formation of compact massive relic galaxies in MOND" (Eappen & Kroupa 2024), focused on the formation of compact massive relic galaxies â€” ancient, compact ellipticals that formed early in the universe and have remained relatively unchanged. My models reproduced key properties of these enigmatic galaxies, including high central velocity dispersions and small effective radii, without invoking mergers or exotic physics.

Finally, in my most recent work, "Scaling relations of early-type galaxies in MOND" (Eappen & Kroupa 2025), I explored the morphological and structural evolution of ETGs across cosmic time. I investigated scaling relations such as the fundamental plane and mass-size relations, finding remarkable agreement with observational data. These results further reinforce MOND as a viable alternative to dark matter-based cosmology at galaxy scales.

Together, these studies paint a coherent picture of galaxy formation and evolution within Milgromian dynamics, offering compelling evidence that challenges the need for dark matter in explaining galaxy-scale phenomena.
